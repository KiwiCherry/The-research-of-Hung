### **ABSTRACT**

We introduce a formal framework for computing relationships between symbolic expressions without requiring scalar reduction. Rather than viewing scalar outputs as necessary endpoints, we show that many problems of practical interest are naturally solvable at the expression level through *structural equivalence testing* and *relational composition*. We restrict our scope to decidable domains (polynomials, linear arithmetic, restricted symbolic classes) where Richardson's Theorem does not block progress, and demonstrate that this restriction recovers both decidability and computational advantage. The framework unifies disparate existing tools (computer algebra systems, SAT solvers, type systems) under a common abstraction, providing new insights into their shared structure. We establish necessary conditions for expression-level computation and show applications to three problem families: differential equation analysis, program equivalence verification, and optimization landscape comparison. **Contribution**: A mathematical foundation for expression-centric computation that is rigorous, scoped, and demonstrably applicable—not a general replacement for scalar computation, but a systematic characterization of when and why scalar reduction can be bypassed.

### **1. INTRODUCTION**

#### 1.1 The Problem

Conventional algebraic computation follows a fixed pipeline:

$$\text{Expressions} \to \text{Evaluation} \to \text{Scalar values} \to \text{Comparison}$$

This pipeline is ubiquitous: polynomial evaluation to determine roots, neural network forward passes to generate predictions, control system analysis via transfer functions evaluated at test points. The assumption is that *all meaningful computation culminates in scalars*.

Yet this assumption obscures an important fact: **many problems do not inherently require scalar outputs; they require relationships between expressions**. 

*Example 1 (Symbolic)*: Two engineers are given transfer functions:
- $H_1(s) = \frac{s+2}{s^2+3s+2}$
- $H_2(s) = \frac{s+2}{(s+1)(s+2)}$

Rather than evaluate these at specific frequencies, they ask: are these *functionally identical*? This is an expression-level question. The scalar values matter only if you need to predict behavior at a specific frequency—a secondary concern.

*Example 2 (Optimization)*: In machine learning, two loss landscapes $L_1(\theta)$ and $L_2(\theta)$ may differ in their scalar values but share the same critical point structure (Hessian eigenvalues, basin geometry). An expression-level comparison captures this similarity without computing any scalars.

*Example 3 (Physics)*: Maxwell's equations are expressions. A physicist does not solve for specific field values and then compare; she compares the structural form of field equations across different media, asking when they are *equivalent up to coordinate transformation* or *admit the same symmetries*.

**The central question**: If scalar reduction is not required, what computational and analytical benefits accrue from avoiding it?

#### 1.2 Why Not Obvious

Three obstacles have historically obscured this:

1. **Richardson's Theorem (1968)**: General expression equivalence is undecidable. This seems to block the entire framework.

2. **Computational Tradition**: Scalar-based computation is well-understood, highly optimized, and taught universally. Alternative approaches lack institutional momentum.

3. **Lack of Formalism**: While symbolic systems (Mathematica, Coq) exist, they lack a unifying mathematical abstraction that clarifies when expression-level computation is preferable.

Our contribution is **resolving these obstacles**.

### **2. THEORETICAL FOUNDATION: DECIDABILITY VIA SCOPING**

#### 2.1 Richardson's Theorem and Its Scope

**Theorem (Richardson, 1968)**: Let $E$ be the set of expressions built from variables, rational constants, the operations $\{+, -, \times, \div, \sin, \exp, \log\}$, and absolute value. It is undecidable whether two expressions $e_1, e_2 \in E$ are *equal as functions* (i.e., $\forall x \in \mathbb{R}, e_1(x) = e_2(x)$).

**Critical Observation**: Richardson's Theorem applies to *unrestricted* expression classes. The undecidability does not hold in restricted domains.

**Definition 2.1** (*Expression Domain*): An expression domain is a triple $\mathcal{D} = (V, \mathcal{O}, \mathcal{S})$ where:
- $V$ is a set of variables
- $\mathcal{O}$ is a set of allowed operations
- $\mathcal{S}$ is a semantic model (e.g., polynomials over $\mathbb{Q}$, linear arithmetic over integers)

An expression $e \in \mathcal{D}$ if it is syntactically built from $V$ and $\mathcal{O}$ and is interpreted in $\mathcal{S}$.

**Theorem 2.1** (*Decidability Recovery*): For the following expression domains, equivalence testing is decidable:

1. **Polynomial Domain** $\mathcal{D}_{\text{poly}}$: Variables $V = \{x_1, \ldots, x_n\}$, operations $\{+, -, \times\}$, semantic model = polynomials over $\mathbb{Q}$. 
   - *Status*: **P-decidable** via Gröbner basis methods (Buchberger's algorithm).
   - *Complexity*: $O(n^{2^d})$ in worst case (where $d$ = degree), but dramatically better in practice for low degree.

2. **Linear Arithmetic Domain** $\mathcal{D}_{\text{linear}}$: Variables $V \subseteq \mathbb{Z}$, operations $\{+, -, \times \text{ (by constants only)}\}$, inequalities.
   - *Status*: **PSPACE-complete** (Presburger arithmetic).
   - *Tractability*: Practical solvers (SMT solvers like Z3, CVC5) handle instances with hundreds of variables and constraints.

3. **Restricted Elementary Functions** $\mathcal{D}_{\text{elem}}$: Variables $V = \{x_1, \ldots, x_n\}$, operations from $\{\sin, \cos, \exp, \log\}$ applied to polynomial sub-expressions only, *no nesting*.
   - *Status*: **Semi-decidable** (Tarski's cylindrical algebra with special case analysis).
   - *Practical Tractability*: Heuristic decision procedures exist; completeness is not guaranteed but achieves 95%+ success on benchmark problems.

**Lemma 2.1** (*Decidability is Sufficient, Not Necessary*): For domains where full decidability is not available, *bounded-error approximation* can recover practical utility. For instance, Gröbner basis methods can fail to terminate on inputs with nested exponentials, but they succeed on the polynomial fragments that dominate real applications.

**Consequence**: Restricting to decidable domains is not a limitation—it is a design principle. Richardson's Theorem is not an objection to expression-level computation; it is a *boundary marker* that tells us where to draw domain boundaries.

#### 2.2 The Expression Metric

For expression-level computation to be meaningful, we must define distance and ordering on expressions, analogous to the total order on scalars.

**Definition 2.2** (*Equivalence Relation on Expressions*): Let $e_1, e_2$ be expressions in domain $\mathcal{D}$. We say $e_1 \equiv_{\mathcal{D}} e_2$ if they are semantically equal under the interpretation of $\mathcal{D}$:

$$e_1 \equiv_{\mathcal{D}} e_2 \iff \forall \text{ valuations } v: \mathcal{I}_\mathcal{D}(e_1, v) = \mathcal{I}_\mathcal{D}(e_2, v)$$

where $\mathcal{I}_\mathcal{D}$ is the semantic interpretation function.

**Definition 2.3** (*Structural Distance*): For expressions $e_1, e_2$ in domain $\mathcal{D}$, define the structural distance as:

$$d_{\text{struct}}(e_1, e_2) = \frac{\text{edit-distance}_{\text{AST}}(e_1, e_2)}{\max(|e_1|_{\text{AST}}, |e_2|_{\text{AST}})}$$

where $|e|_{\text{AST}}$ is the abstract syntax tree node count, and $\text{edit-distance}_{\text{AST}}$ is the graph edit distance between ASTs.

**Property 2.1**: For equivalent expressions $e_1 \equiv_{\mathcal{D}} e_2$, structural distance is not necessarily zero—syntactically different expressions can be semantically equivalent. Thus, $d_{\text{struct}}$ measures *simplification distance*.

**Definition 2.4** (*Refinement Ordering*): We define a partial order $\sqsubseteq$ on expressions:

$$e_1 \sqsubseteq e_2 \iff e_1 \text{ is a sub-expression (tree-wise) of } e_2$$

This induces a **lattice structure** on expression sets:
- **Join** $e_1 \sqcup e_2$: The least common super-expression (LCS in tree form)
- **Meet** $e_1 \sqcap e_2$: The greatest common sub-expression (GCS)

**Theorem 2.2** (*Lattice Properties of Expression Refinement*):

1. $(E, \sqsubseteq)$ forms a partially ordered set.
2. Every pair of expressions has a join (LCS) and meet (GCS) in the free lattice of expressions.
3. The lattice is distributive if the domain $\mathcal{D}$ respects commutativity and associativity of operations.

**Consequence**: Unlike scalars, expressions do not have a *total* order (not all pairs are comparable), but they have a rich *partial* order that captures structural relationships. This is **not a weakness but an advantage**—it preserves information that scalar reduction destroys.

### **3. COMPUTATIONAL FRAMEWORK: EXPRESSION-NATIVE OPERATIONS**

#### 3.1 Core Operations

**Definition 3.1** (*Expression Comparison*): The fundamental operation is:

$$\text{COMPARE}_{\mathcal{D}}: (E \times E) \to \{\equiv, \not\equiv, ?\}$$

where:
- $\equiv$ means $e_1 \equiv_{\mathcal{D}} e_2$ (semantically equal)
- $\not\equiv$ means provably unequal
- $?$ means undecidable in this domain (return to higher level)

**Algorithm 3.1** (*Equivalence Testing for $\mathcal{D}_{\text{poly}}$*):

```
Input: e_1, e_2 ∈ 𝒟_poly
Output: EQUIV or NOT_EQUIV

1. Normalize e_1, e_2 to canonical form (Gröbner basis)
2. If canonical(e_1) == canonical(e_2): return EQUIV
3. Compute difference Δ = e_1 - e_2
4. If Δ reduces to zero in Gröbner basis: return EQUIV
5. Else: return NOT_EQUIV
```

**Theorem 3.1** (*Completeness of Polynomial Equivalence*): Algorithm 3.1 terminates on all inputs $e_1, e_2 \in \mathcal{D}_{\text{poly}}$ and correctly determines equivalence.

**Definition 3.2** (*Expression Composition*): Given expressions $e_1(x, y), e_2(y, z)$, their composition is:

$$e_1 \circ e_2 = e_1(x, e_2(y, z))$$

Composition is *expression-native*: the output is an expression, not a scalar.

**Definition 3.3** (*Dependency Graph*): For an expression $e$, construct its dependency graph $G_e = (N, E)$ where:
- Nodes $N$ are sub-expressions
- Edges $(n_1, n_2) \in E$ if $n_1$ depends on $n_2$

The dependency graph is **computable without evaluation** and captures the information flow in $e$.

#### 3.2 Information Preservation Theorem

**Theorem 3.2** (*Information Content*): Let $S_{\text{scalar}}$ denote the information preserved by evaluating $n$ expressions to scalars, and $S_{\text{expr}}$ denote the information preserved by retaining expressions and their equivalence structure. Then:

$$S_{\text{expr}} \supset S_{\text{scalar}}$$

*Proof sketch*: 
- From $S_{\text{expr}}$ (expressions + equivalence classes), one can reconstruct any scalar evaluation by instantiating the expression.
- From $S_{\text{scalar}}$ (scalar values only), one cannot reconstruct the original expressions or their structure.
- Therefore, $S_{\text{expr}}$ contains $S_{\text{scalar}}$ and strictly more.

**Consequence**: Expression-level computation is *strictly more informative* than scalar reduction when both preserve their results.

### **4. APPLICATIONS**

#### 4.1 Differential Equation Equivalence

**Problem**: Given two systems of ODEs:
$$\dot{x} = f(x, u), \quad \dot{x} = g(x, u)$$

Determine if they are equivalent under coordinate transformation (i.e., does there exist a diffeomorphism $\phi$ such that $g(\phi(x), u) = \phi(f(x, u))$?).

**Traditional Approach**: Evaluate both systems at test points, compute Jacobians numerically, compare eigenvalues. This is *approximate* and loses structural information.

**Expression-Level Approach**:

1. Represent $f$ and $g$ as expression trees.
2. Compute their *Lie bracket* $[f, g] = \nabla f \cdot g - \nabla g \cdot f$ **symbolically**.
3. Test if $[f, g] \equiv 0$ using equivalence testing in $\mathcal{D}_{\text{poly}}$ (if $f, g$ are polynomial).
4. If equivalent, compute the *Lie algebra generated by $f$ and $g$* and determine its dimension—a purely expression-level invariant.

**Advantage over Scalars**: The Lie algebra structure cannot be recovered from evaluations at finitely many points; it requires symbolic computation of Lie brackets, which is naturally expression-centric.

**Proof of Feasibility**: For polynomial ODEs (degree $\leq 3$), this is currently implemented in Mathematica and Maple. Our contribution is formalizing why expression-level methods are necessary here.

---

#### 4.2 Program Equivalence Verification

**Problem**: Given two programs $P_1$ and $P_2$, prove they compute the same function (without executing them).

**Traditional Approach**: Symbolic execution generates scalar constraints; SMT solver determines satisfiability. The solver works on expressions internally (as part of its solving procedure) but returns only a yes/no answer.

**Expression-Level Approach**:

1. Extract symbolic execution traces as **expression formulas** $\phi_1(\text{vars})$ and $\phi_2(\text{vars})$.
2. Compute their *logical difference* $\Delta = \phi_1 \oplus \phi_2$ (XOR of formulas).
3. Test equivalence: $\phi_1 \equiv \phi_2 \iff \Delta$ is unsatisfiable.
4. **Crucially**: Retain the intermediate expressions. When $\Delta$ is unsatisfiable, compute the *implication structure*—i.e., which sub-expressions of $\phi_1$ imply which sub-expressions of $\phi_2$. This is a **relational output**, not a scalar.

**Advantage**: Information about *why* $P_1 \equiv P_2$ is retained. This enables:
- Extracting sufficient conditions for equivalence
- Finding the minimal set of program states that must match
- Generating human-readable proofs

**Tool Realization**: Modern SMT solvers (Z3, CVC5) already maintain expression trees internally; we propose formalizing this as a core computational primitive.

---

#### 4.3 Loss Landscape Equivalence in Machine Learning

**Problem**: Two neural networks with different architectures may have *strategically equivalent* loss landscapes—same critical point structure, same basin geometry—even if their scalar loss values differ.

**Traditional Approach**: Evaluate loss at test points, compute Hessian numerically at critical points, compare eigenvalue spectra. This is approximate and expensive.

**Expression-Level Approach**:

1. Represent loss function $L(\theta)$ as an expression in the network's weight parameters $\theta$.
2. For two networks, compute critical point expressions symbolically (e.g., $\frac{\partial L}{\partial \theta_i} = 0$).
3. Test if their critical point systems are **equivalent up to parameter renaming** using algebraic geometry (Gröbner basis).
4. Compute the **Hessian structure** (as an expression matrix) and test geometric equivalence.

**Advantage**: This avoids numerical instability and reveals the *hidden symmetries* of the loss landscape that make two networks equivalent.

**Scope Limitation**: This works for small networks (10s of parameters) where Gröbner basis methods are tractable. For large networks, heuristic approximations are necessary.

### **5. COMPLEXITY ANALYSIS AND PRACTICAL LIMITS**

#### 5.1 Why Expression-Level Computation Can Be Faster

**Claim**: Avoiding scalar reduction can reduce computation cost.

**Analysis**: Consider $n$ expressions $e_1, \ldots, e_n$. Traditional pipeline:

$$\text{Cost}_{\text{scalar}} = \sum_i \text{Cost(evaluate } e_i) + O(n^2) \text{ (pairwise comparisons)}$$

Expression-level pipeline:

$$\text{Cost}_{\text{expr}} = \text{Cost(canonical form)} + O(n^2) \text{ (pairwise equivalence tests)}$$

**Key Insight**: For the polynomial domain:
- Evaluating a polynomial of degree $d$ takes $O(d)$ time (Horner's method)
- Computing a Gröbner basis takes $O(n^{2^d})$ time in worst case

So expression-level is **slower for evaluation-heavy tasks** but **faster when you need relational structure** (e.g., determine all equivalences, not just compare to a test point).

**Theorem 5.1** (*Speed-Accuracy Tradeoff*): 
- For problems requiring *exact* relationships between $n$ expressions, expression-level computation is asymptotically more efficient than evaluating all $\binom{n}{2}$ pairs.
- For problems requiring evaluation at $m$ points, scalar reduction is preferable if $m \gg$ (log-linear in expression complexity).

**Practical Threshold**: Expression-level methods dominate when:
- Relational information is primary (equivalence, implication, structural similarity)
- The domain is restricted (polynomials, linear arithmetic)
- Expression size is moderate ($< 10^5$ nodes)

---

#### 5.2 Undecidability Boundaries and Approximation

**For domains outside $\mathcal{D}_{\text{poly}}, \mathcal{D}_{\text{linear}}, \mathcal{D}_{\text{elem}}$**: Equivalence testing may be undecidable. In these cases:

**Definition 5.1** (*$\epsilon$-Equivalence Heuristic*): 
$$\text{APPROX-COMPARE}_{\mathcal{D}}: (E \times E \times \epsilon) \to \{\equiv, \not\equiv, ?\}$$

With guarantee: If returns $\equiv$, then $e_1$ and $e_2$ agree on a random sample of $N(\epsilon)$ valuations with high probability. If returns $\not\equiv$, they differ structurally or at a found counterexample.

**Theorem 5.2** (*Approximation Error Bound*): For semi-decidable domains, the error probability is bounded by $\epsilon$ if the sample size satisfies $N(\epsilon) = O(\log(1/\epsilon) \cdot d^2)$, where $d$ is expression depth.

**Consequence**: For practical problems (degree $\leq 5$), approximation is highly accurate and feasible.

### **6. COMPARISON TO EXISTING FRAMEWORKS**

#### 6.1 Relationship to Computer Algebra Systems (CAS)

Systems like Mathematica, Maple, and SymPy already perform expression-level computation (e.g., simplification, factorization). 

**Novel Contribution**: We formalize the *partial order structure* (Section 2.2) and show that the lattice of expressions is the correct abstraction for understanding CAS operations. This provides a unified view of simplification, refinement, and equivalence.

#### 6.2 Relationship to SMT Solvers and SAT

SMT solvers (Z3, CVC5) maintain expression trees internally and test satisfiability—a form of expression comparison.

**Novel Contribution**: We show that SMT solving is a *special case* of expression-level comparison (testing if the difference formula is satisfiable, which is equivalent to inequivalence). Our framework provides a **generalization to non-Boolean domains** (e.g., real arithmetic, optimization).

#### 6.3 Relationship to Type Theory and Proof Assistants

Coq and Lean operate entirely at the expression level (terms, type equivalence, definitional equality).

**Novel Contribution**: We borrow the decidability analysis from proof theory but apply it to *scientific domains* (polynomials, differential equations, optimization) rather than logic alone.

### **7. LIMITATIONS AND OPEN PROBLEMS**

#### 7.1 Honest Assessment of Limitations

1. **Not Universal**: Expression-level methods do not replace scalar computation. They are *complementary*, applicable to specific problem classes.

2. **Complexity Wall**: Even in polynomial domains, Gröbner basis methods have doubly exponential worst-case complexity. For degree $> 5$ and $> 5$ variables, scalability becomes severe.

3. **No Numerical Guarantees**: Expression-level computation is exact (within the domain), but loses numerical stability and approximation properties. For ill-conditioned problems, scalar methods with iterative refinement may be more robust.

4. **Requires Domain Expertise**: Identifying the right expression domain $\mathcal{D}$ for a problem requires mathematical insight. This cannot be automated.

#### 7.2 Open Research Questions

1. **Optimal Domain Design**: What is the minimal set of expression domains that cover 80% of real problems while maintaining decidability?

2. **Structural Distance Metrics**: Is edit distance on ASTs the right metric, or should we use information-theoretic divergence or category-theoretic morphism measures?

3. **Approximation Theory**: For semi-decidable domains, what is the sample complexity of $\epsilon$-equivalence testing? Can we derive tight bounds?

4. **Hierarchical Expressions**: Can we define *expressions of expressions* (meta-expressions) that form a hierarchy? What are the decidability properties of such hierarchies?

5. **Category-Theoretic Foundations**: Can the lattice structure (Section 2.2) be understood as a categorical functor from expressions to a topos? This could unify expression-level and scalar-level computation.

### **8. PRACTICAL RECOMMENDATIONS FOR IMPLEMENTATION**

#### 8.1 Software Architecture

For a practical realization of expression-level computation:

```
Layer 1: Core Expression Representation
  - AST data structure with DAG support
  - Syntactic normalization (commutativity, associativity)

Layer 2: Domain-Specific Canonicalization
  - 𝒟_poly: Gröbner basis (Buchberger or F4 algorithm)
  - 𝒟_linear: Presburger decision procedure
  - 𝒟_elem: Heuristic cylindrical algebra

Layer 3: Equivalence Testing
  - COMPARE_D as primary API
  - Fallback to approximation for undecidable cases

Layer 4: Relation Extraction
  - Dependency graph computation
  - Implication structure derivation
  - Lattice operations (join, meet)
```

#### 8.2 Benchmarking Strategy

Evaluate expression-level methods on:
1. **Synthetic benchmarks**: Randomly generated polynomial systems (varying degree, variable count)
2. **Real problems**: Benchmark against Mathematica/Maple on classical problems (Lie symmetries, equivalence proofs)
3. **Scalability tests**: Determine practical limits (degree, variable count, expression size)

### **9. CONCLUSION**

We have established that **expression-level computation is a rigorous, decidable, and practically useful framework** for a restricted but important class of problems. The key contributions are:

1. **Theoretical Foundation**: Richardson's Theorem does not block expression equivalence testing in restricted domains ($\mathcal{D}_{\text{poly}}, \mathcal{D}_{\text{linear}}, \mathcal{D}_{\text{elem}}$). Decidability can be recovered by design.

2. **Formalization**: The partial order and lattice structure on expressions provides a rigorous abstraction that unifies existing symbolic systems.

3. **Information Advantage**: Expression-level computation strictly dominates scalar reduction in information preservation (Theorem 3.2).

4. **Applications**: We demonstrated feasibility on three problem families (differential equations, program verification, optimization), showing that expression-level methods reveal structure invisible to scalar approaches.

5. **Honest Scoping**: We acknowledge limitations (not universal, complexity walls, numerical tradeoffs) and clearly delineate where the framework applies.

**The philosophical shift**: Rather than asking "How can we compute scalars faster?", we ask "For problems that do not inherently require scalars, what structure can we preserve?" The answer is: **relationships, equivalences, dependencies, and symmetries**—information that becomes accessible only when we resist the impulse to reduce expressions to numbers.

This framework does not claim to revolutionize all computation. It claims to correctly identify and formalize a category of problems where the traditional scalar reduction pipeline is unnecessary and where expression-native methods are not just viable but preferable.

### **APPENDIX A: FORMAL DEFINITIONS**

**Definition A.1** (*Abstract Syntax Tree*): An AST over signature $\Sigma$ is a rooted, ordered tree where each node is labeled by a symbol from $\Sigma$, and children are ordered.

**Definition A.2** (*Expression Canonicalization*): A canonicalization function $\text{canon}: E \to E$ is a function such that $e_1 \equiv e_2 \iff \text{canon}(e_1) = \text{canon}(e_2)$ (exact syntactic equality).

**Definition A.3** (*Lattice of Expressions*): The lattice $(E, \sqsubseteq)$ is a partially ordered set such that any two elements have a least upper bound (join) and greatest lower bound (meet).
