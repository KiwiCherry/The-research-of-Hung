I think, if theory is about bypassing convolution operations:

This needs extreme precision:

Define the input-output relationship you care about (what semantic property must be preserved?)

Show that this property is a verifiable structural invariant (can be checked without arithmetic)

Prove that inputs with identical structural invariants have identical semantic meaning

Benchmark your method vs. standard convolution on real data

Acknowledge: "This applies to problems where [specific condition]. For general convolution, [existing method] remains optimal."

What the Paper (a2) Actually Says

Read Section 4.3 carefully:

"Common pitfall 1: 'We use a compact representation, therefore we've reduced complexity.' Truth: You've changed the representation. Complexity may move elsewhere."

This is not saying your idea is impossible. It's saying:

You must measure total cost, including representation.

If you can show:

Total cost is actually lower (measured empirically on realistic hardware)

For a well-defined problem class (not universal)

Compared to the best baseline (not theoretical bounds)

With honest accounting of trade-offs

Then you don't get demolished—you get published.
