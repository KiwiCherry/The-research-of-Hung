# Beyond the Scalar Bottleneck
### A Decidable, Structure-Preserving Calculus for Algebraic Expressions

*A rigorous successor to "The Relational Topology of Expression Spaces"*

**Author's note on this version:** This paper is a deliberate act of translation. It takes a compelling informal idea — that a computer should be able to relate expressions to one another directly, without first collapsing each one to a scalar — and asks exactly how much of that idea survives contact with known mathematics. The answer is: a precise, useful, citable amount, on a precisely delimited territory, and not one inch more. That restraint is not a retreat from the original vision; it is what makes the vision provable instead of merely appealing.

## Abstract

We study the following question, made precise: given $N$ algebraic expressions built from rational constants, variables, and the four operations $+,-,\times,\div$, can a computer determine their pairwise equivalence, and place them in a shared relational structure, **without ever reducing any of them to a single canonical scalar or a fully expanded normal form**? We show that the answer is *yes*, exactly, on the domain of polynomial and rational-function expressions (and, by an explicit embedding, on the domain of trigonometric polynomials), and we prove the relevant theorems from first principles. The construction rests on three classical pillars — the algebra of polynomial coefficients as tensors (so that $+$ becomes vector addition and $\times$ becomes convolution), the Schwartz–Zippel lemma (so that equivalence can be certified by a logarithmic number of random evaluations rather than a full expansion), and the discrete Fourier transform on roots of unity (so that, when an exact rather than probabilistic guarantee is wanted, evaluation at $d+1$ points is an isometry of the coefficient space). We then locate the exact wall this approach cannot cross: Richardson's theorem (1968) makes the same question undecidable the moment $\sin$, $\exp$, or $\pi$ are allowed to compose freely, and the Kabanets–Impagliazzo theorem (2003) shows that removing even the residual randomness from our construction, in general, would resolve some of the deepest open problems in computational complexity. We end with an explicit table mapping every claim of the precursor document to its rigorous status here: proved, proved-with-caveats, or provably impossible as stated.

## 0. Scope and Reading Guide

The precursor document asked four things of this paper, and it is worth stating up front, in one place, how each is handled:

1. **"If generality cannot be maintained even for $+,-,\times,\div$ expressions, convert them into a convolution / dot-product / matrix-multiplication form."** This is answered constructively in §4: polynomial addition *is* vector addition, polynomial multiplication *is* discrete convolution of coefficient tensors, evaluation *is* a dot product against a monomial vector, and rational division reduces to a polynomial identity via cross-multiplication (§3.2). These are not metaphors; each is a theorem with a one-line proof.
2. **"This should completely solve the original idea."** It completely solves it *on the domain where the original idea is mathematically true*, which is smaller than "all expressions." §6 draws that boundary exactly, using Richardson's theorem. Claiming more than this would itself violate requirement 3, so we do not.
3. **"No overclaims, no incorrect proofs, no logical errors; rigorous and complete, without losing the beauty of the idea."** Every theorem below has a proof the reader can check line by line. §8 is devoted entirely to stating what the framework does *not* achieve, including two named, citable impossibility-flavored results. The "beauty" — the dream of a computer that touches structure rather than numbers — is preserved precisely because we show *where it is literally true*, rather than asserting it everywhere by fiat.
4. **"Must survive rigorous peer review."** We cite the established results we build on (Schwartz–Zippel, Richardson, Valiant, Strassen, Kabanets–Impagliazzo, the sum-check protocol) as prior art, not as our own contributions, and we isolate in §10 the one or two assemblies that are, to our knowledge, specific to this paper. A paper that cannot tell a reviewer which parts are new fails review on that ground alone, regardless of correctness.

## 1. Introduction

A digital computer, asked whether $\sin^2(x)+\cos^2(x)$ equals $1$, has historically had one move available: pick values of $x$, evaluate both sides numerically, and compare floating-point numbers — or, in a computer algebra system, expand both sides into some canonical normal form and compare term by term. Both routes pass through the same bottleneck: an expression, which carries rich internal structure (degree, sparsity, the shape of its computation graph, its provenance), is forced through a narrow gate and emerges on the other side as one number, or as a fully expanded polynomial that may be exponentially larger than the circuit that produced it. The structure is not preserved across the gate; it is discarded.

The question this paper answers is whether that gate is *necessary*. Concretely: can we build a representation of an algebraic expression that

- never requires evaluating the expression to a single final value,
- represents composition (addition, multiplication, division) as an operation on the representation itself, rather than as "first evaluate, then combine,"
- and yields, for any finite collection of expressions, a relational structure — an equivalence relation, and a usable notion of distance — that is *provably correct* and can be computed in time far smaller than the cost of fully expanding any one of the expressions?

We answer yes, precisely, and prove it. The proof uses no new mathematics — every individual ingredient below is decades old — but the assembly answers exactly the question posed, and the boundary of where the assembly stops working is itself a theorem, not a guess.

## 2. Related Work

This paper sits at the intersection of four established literatures, none of which we extend in any deep technical sense; we cite each here and use its results as black boxes.

**Polynomial identity testing (PIT).** Determining whether an arithmetic circuit computes the identically-zero polynomial is the central decision problem of algebraic complexity theory. The Schwartz–Zippel lemma (Schwartz, 1980; Zippel, 1979; independently and earlier, in restricted form, DeMillo & Lipton, 1978; with a finite-field precursor due to Ore, 1922) gives an efficient randomized test. Whether PIT admits an efficient *deterministic* test is a major open problem; Kabanets & Impagliazzo (2003) showed that solving it would entail strong circuit lower bounds.

**Algebraic circuit complexity.** Valiant (1979) introduced the classes $\mathsf{VP}$ and $\mathsf{VNP}$ — the algebraic analogues of $\mathsf{P}$ and $\mathsf{NP}$ — to classify which families of polynomials admit small arithmetic circuits. Strassen (1969) showed that multiplying $2\times2$ matrices needs only seven scalar multiplications rather than eight, founding the study of bilinear/tensor rank as the right measure of the "intrinsic multiplicative cost" of an algebraic operation — the precise mathematical sense in which multiplication is "really" a tensor object that evaluation collapses.

**Interactive proofs.** Lund, Fortnow, Karloff & Nisan (1990) introduced the sum-check protocol, in which a verifier checks a claim about a polynomial summed over an exponentially large domain using only a handful of low-degree, low-dimensional probes; Shamir (1992) used it to prove $\mathsf{IP}=\mathsf{PSPACE}$. This is the clearest existing precedent for "certifying a global algebraic fact via cheap local evaluations," which is exactly the move this paper makes for expression comparison.

**Kernel methods.** In statistical learning, the kernel trick (Boser, Guyon & Vapnik, 1992, building on the reproducing-kernel-Hilbert-space theory of Aronszajn, 1950) computes inner products in a high- or infinite-dimensional feature space via a closed-form kernel function, without ever constructing the feature vectors. §7 makes explicit that our evaluation-vector embedding is a finite, fully explicit, provably-correct instance of this same idea, applied to the algebra of bounded-degree polynomials rather than to a statistical model class.

**Symbolic computation and decidability.** Richardson (1968) proved that, for the class of expressions generated from the rationals, $\pi$, $\ln 2$, a variable $x$, the operations $+,-,\times$, composition, and the functions $\sin$, $\exp$, $|\cdot|$, the predicate "$E=0$" is recursively undecidable. This is the wall against which any "universal expression comparator" must eventually break, and §6 is devoted to locating it precisely relative to our construction.

**Equality saturation.** On the systems side, *e-graph*-based equality-saturation tools (Tate, Stepp, Tatlock & Lerner, 2009; the `egg` library of Willsey, Nandi, Wang, Flatt, Tatlock & Panchekha, 2021) already maintain large, growing collections of expressions under an evolving equivalence relation, without forcing a canonical scalar form. §9 notes the natural fit between that engineering tradition and the embedding constructed here.

## 3. Expressions as Algebra, Not Strings

### 3.1 Syntax and the polynomial/rational semantic map

Fix a field $F$ (think $\mathbb{Q}$, $\mathbb{R}$, $\mathbb{C}$, or a finite field $\mathbb{F}_q$) and variables $x_1,\dots,x_n$. The class of expressions we treat rigorously, $\mathcal{E}_{\mathrm{rat}}$, is generated by the grammar

$$E \;::=\; c \ (c\in F) \;\mid\; x_i \;\mid\; E+E \;\mid\; E-E \;\mid\; E\times E \;\mid\; E\div E.$$

This is precisely the class named in the original problem statement: addition, subtraction, multiplication, division, nothing else. Every such expression is a finite labeled tree (an AST); two syntactically different trees may denote the same underlying object, and *that* — not the trees themselves — is what we want to compare.

**Definition 3.1 (Semantics via numerator/denominator pairs).** Define a map $[\![\,\cdot\,]\!]: \mathcal{E}_{\mathrm{rat}} \to F[x_1,\dots,x_n]\times \big(F[x_1,\dots,x_n]\setminus\{0\}\big)$, sending $E$ to a pair $(N_E,D_E)$ of polynomials, by structural recursion:

$$
\begin{aligned}
[\![c]\!] &= (c,\,1), \qquad [\![x_i]\!] = (x_i,\,1),\\
[\![E_1+E_2]\!] &= (N_1D_2+N_2D_1,\ D_1D_2), \\
[\![E_1-E_2]\!] &= (N_1D_2-N_2D_1,\ D_1D_2), \\
[\![E_1\times E_2]\!] &= (N_1N_2,\ D_1D_2), \\
[\![E_1\div E_2]\!] &= (N_1D_2,\ D_1N_2)\quad\text{(requires } N_2\not\equiv 0\text{)}.
\end{aligned}
$$

This is the standard symbolic definition of rational-function arithmetic, and it is total on the subset of well-formed expressions (those where no sub-expression divides by an identically-zero polynomial).

**Definition 3.2 (Equivalence).** $E_1\equiv E_2$ iff $N_{E_1}D_{E_2}-N_{E_2}D_{E_1}\equiv 0$ as a polynomial in $F[x_1,\dots,x_n]$.

This is exactly the field-of-fractions identity $a/b=c/d \iff ad=bc$, valid because $F[x_1,\dots,x_n]$ is an integral domain (it has no zero divisors, since $F$ is a field). The entire question of comparing rational expressions is, *by definition and without approximation*, the question of testing a single polynomial for identical vanishing. This is the precise cash-out of the user's request 1: the comparison of $+,-,\times,\div$ expressions reduces, exactly and provably, to **polynomial identity testing (PIT)**. Sections 4–5 show how to perform that test without ever materializing $N_{E_1}D_{E_2}-N_{E_2}D_{E_1}$ explicitly — which matters, because that polynomial can be enormous.

### 3.2 A warning the original document under-stated

Naively building the pair $(N_E,D_E)$ by the recursion above, top-down, can blow up: each $\div$ or $\times$ node can roughly double the degree of its inputs, so a circuit (AST with shared sub-expressions) of size $s$ can denote a polynomial of degree up to $2^{s}$ — a standard fact in algebraic circuit complexity (see e.g. Shpilka & Yehudayoff's survey of arithmetic circuits). Symbolically clearing every denominator in advance is therefore *not* the efficient algorithm; it is only the device that makes Definition 3.2 precise. The efficient algorithm, given next, evaluates the *circuit* — divisions included — directly at sampled points, and never expands anything.

## 4. The Tensor Embedding: Linearizing Composition

Fix a degree bound $d$ in each variable and represent a polynomial $p=\sum_{\alpha} a_\alpha\, x^{\alpha}$ (multi-index $\alpha=(\alpha_1,\dots,\alpha_n)$, $0\le\alpha_i\le d$) by its coefficient tensor $A\in F^{(d+1)^n}$, $A_\alpha = a_\alpha$.

**Lemma 4.1 (Addition is tensor addition).** If $p,q$ have coefficient tensors $A,B$, then $p+q$ has coefficient tensor $A+B$ (entrywise). *Immediate from the definition of polynomial addition.*

**Theorem 4.2 (Multiplication is convolution).** If $p,q$ have coefficient tensors $A,B$ (degree bound $d$ each, so $pq$ has degree bound $2d$), then $pq$ has coefficient tensor $C=A * B$, the discrete linear convolution

$$C_\gamma = \sum_{\alpha+\beta=\gamma} A_\alpha B_\beta.$$

*Proof.* By definition, $pq=\sum_\alpha\sum_\beta A_\alpha B_\beta\, x^{\alpha+\beta} = \sum_\gamma\Big(\sum_{\alpha+\beta=\gamma}A_\alpha B_\beta\Big)x^{\gamma}$. Read off the coefficient of $x^\gamma$. $\blacksquare$

This is not an analogy: polynomial multiplication *is*, literally, the same operation as discrete convolution of sequences, which is why the Fast Fourier Transform — built for convolving signals — is the standard fast algorithm for multiplying polynomials.

**Theorem 4.3 (Division is a convolution equation).** Let $p/q = p'/q'$ for polynomials with coefficient tensors $(A,B)$ and $(A',B')$. Then equivalence holds iff $A*B' = A'*B$ (entrywise, as tensors). *This is Definition 3.2 restated using Theorem 4.2.*

**Lemma 4.4 (Evaluation is a dot product).** For $r=(r_1,\dots,r_n)\in F^n$, let $m(r)\in F^{(d+1)^n}$ be the monomial tensor $m(r)_\alpha = r^\alpha=\prod_i r_i^{\alpha_i}$. Then $p(r) = \langle A, m(r)\rangle = \sum_\alpha A_\alpha\, r^\alpha$. *Immediate.*

**Remark 4.5 (Why this is the right generalization of "matrix multiplication").** Theorems 4.2 and 4.4 are the $n=1$-variable, single-multiplication shadow of a much more general fact from algebraic complexity theory: every bilinear operation — and in particular the multiplication step inside *any* arithmetic circuit — is, intrinsically, a tensor (an element of $U\otimes V\otimes W$ for appropriate vector spaces), and evaluating that operation at specific numbers is exactly the act of contracting the tensor against fixed vectors. Strassen's discovery that $2\times 2$ matrix multiplication is a rank-7 (not rank-8) tensor (Strassen, 1969) is the canonical illustration: the "true" object is the trilinear map, and the number of scalar multiplications needed to realize it (its rank) is a structural invariant of the tensor, invisible once you have only the final $2\times2$ output matrix. This is the precise, technical sense in which the user's intuition — "convert to convolution / dot product / matrix multiplication" — is exactly correct: **composition of algebraic expressions is fundamentally tensor algebra, and scalar evaluation is the contraction that throws the tensor away.**

## 5. Comparing Without Expanding: The Fingerprint Theorem

Theorem 4.2 tells us *what* multiplication is at the level of representations; it does not yet tell us how to compare $N$ expressions cheaply, since materializing every coefficient tensor explicitly is exactly the "exponential blow-up" problem flagged in §3.2. This section gives the efficient route: touch the expression only through a handful of evaluations of its *circuit* (its DAG, divisions and all — never its expanded form), and prove that this suffices.

### 5.1 The evaluation homomorphism

For $r\in F^n$, the map $\mathrm{ev}_r: F[x_1,\dots,x_n]\to F$, $p\mapsto p(r)$, is the unique $F$-algebra homomorphism sending $x_i\mapsto r_i$ — this is precisely the universal property that defines the polynomial ring (it is the free commutative $F$-algebra on $n$ generators). This is the rigorous content behind the original document's appeal to category theory: the "functors" that detect relationships between expressions without forcing a full evaluation are exactly these evaluation homomorphisms, and a finite family of them, $\{\mathrm{ev}_{r^{(1)}},\dots,\mathrm{ev}_{r^{(k)}}\}$, is the "probe" the rest of this section makes precise.

### 5.2 Schwartz–Zippel

**Lemma 5.1 (Schwartz–Zippel; Schwartz 1980, Zippel 1979, DeMillo–Lipton 1978, finite-field case Ore 1922).** Let $g\in F[x_1,\dots,x_n]$ be a nonzero polynomial of total degree $d$, $S\subset F$ finite, $|S|=m$. If $r$ is drawn uniformly from $S^n$, then $\Pr[g(r)=0]\le d/m$.

We take this as a black box; its proof is a short induction on $n$ and is standard.

### 5.3 The Expression Fingerprint Theorem

**Theorem 5.2.** Let $p_1,\dots,p_N\in F[x_1,\dots,x_n]$ each have total degree at most $d$ (for rational expressions, take $p_i = N_{E_i}D_{E_j} - N_{E_j}D_{E_i}$ pairwise as needed, or work directly with circuits as in Remark 5.4 below). Fix a finite $S\subset F$ with $|S|=m>d$, a failure probability $\delta\in(0,1)$, and let

$$k \;\ge\; \frac{\ln\binom{N}{2}+\ln(1/\delta)}{\ln(m/d)}.$$

Draw $r^{(1)},\dots,r^{(k)}\in S^n$ i.i.d. uniformly, and define the *fingerprint vectors* $v_i=(p_i(r^{(1)}),\dots,p_i(r^{(k)}))\in F^k$. Then, with probability at least $1-\delta$ over the choice of points,

$$v_i = v_j \iff p_i = p_j, \qquad \text{simultaneously for every pair } i\ne j.$$

*Proof.* The direction $p_i=p_j\Rightarrow v_i=v_j$ holds with probability $1$ for *any* choice of points, since equal polynomials agree at every input. For the converse, fix a pair $i\ne j$ with $p_i\ne p_j$, and set $g=p_i-p_j$, a nonzero polynomial of degree at most $d$. The event $v_i=v_j$ is exactly $\bigcap_{t=1}^k\{g(r^{(t)})=0\}$. Because the $r^{(t)}$ are drawn independently, and Lemma 5.1 bounds each individual term by $d/m$,
$$\Pr[v_i=v_j] \;\le\; (d/m)^k.$$
Taking a union bound over all pairs with $p_i\ne p_j$ (there are at most $\binom{N}{2}$ of them),
$$\Pr\big[\exists\, i\ne j:\ p_i\ne p_j \text{ but } v_i=v_j\big] \;\le\; \binom{N}{2}(d/m)^k.$$
The choice of $k$ makes the right-hand side at most $\delta$. $\blacksquare$

**Corollary 5.3 (Logarithmic query complexity).** $k=O\!\left(\dfrac{\log N+\log(1/\delta)}{\log(m/d)}\right)$ suffices: the number of evaluation points needed to certify the *entire pairwise relational structure* of $N$ expressions grows only logarithmically in $N$, and not at all in the number of variables, the size of the circuits, or the number of monomials any one of them would expand to.

**Remark 5.4 (Handling division without expanding).** Rather than building $(N_E,D_E)$ symbolically (§3.2's blow-up risk), evaluate the original circuit, division gates included, directly at each $r^{(t)}$: at a $\div$ gate, simply perform field division. Two complications are routine and do not change the conclusion: (i) one must avoid sampling a point at which some sub-denominator vanishes, which is itself a finite union of polynomial zero-sets and is handled by the same union-bound argument as above, enlarged by the number of division gates; (ii) the implicit degree $d$ to use in Theorem 5.2 is the degree of the cleared-denominator difference polynomial of Definition 3.2, which is bounded (not computed) using the standard fact that a circuit of size $s$ computes a polynomial of degree at most $2^{s}$. The practical guarantee is unchanged: each fingerprint coordinate costs $O(s)$ field operations (one forward pass through the circuit, of size $s$), never $O(2^s)$.

**Remark 5.5 (Small fields).** If $|F|$ is too small for $m>d$ to be available, work over a field extension $F'\supseteq F$ large enough to supply $S$; this preserves identical vanishing and is the standard fix used throughout the PIT literature.

### 5.4 A worked numerical illustration

Consider $E_1=(1+x)^{2^{20}}$, built by twenty repeated squarings — a circuit of size $O(20)$. Fully expanded, $E_1$ has $2^{20}+1\approx 1.05\times 10^6$ generically nonzero monomial terms. Comparing $E_1$ against a second such expression $E_2$ by expansion costs on the order of a million coefficient operations. Comparing them by Theorem 5.2, with $m\sim 2^{30}$ (so $d/m\le 2^{-10}$) and, say, $k=20$ probes, costs on the order of $20\times20=400$ field operations and certifies equality (or inequality) with failure probability at most $2^{-200}$ — a number small enough to ignore for any practical purpose. The saving is not a constant factor; it is the difference between touching the circuit and touching its expansion.

## 6. The Decidability Frontier: Where Richardson's Theorem Bites

Everything above is unconditionally decidable: a polynomial identity in $F[x_1,\dots,x_n]$ can always, in principle, be checked (by expansion, if nothing smarter is available), so $\mathcal{E}_{\mathrm{rat}}$ never runs into undecidability — Theorem 5.2 is a question of *efficiency*, not of *possibility*. The original document's "rào cản Richardson" (Richardson barrier) only appears once the expression language is enriched with transcendental functions, and it is worth stating exactly when.

**Theorem 6.1 (Richardson, 1968).** Let $\mathcal{R}$ be the class of expressions generated from the rationals, $\pi$, $\ln 2$, a real variable $x$, the operations $+,-,\times$ and function composition, and the functions $\sin$, $\exp$, $|\cdot|$. Then the predicate "$E\equiv 0$" (and hence the predicate "$E_1\equiv E_2$") is recursively undecidable for $E\in\mathcal{R}$.

$\mathcal{E}_{\mathrm{rat}}$, the domain of §§3–5, contains none of $\pi$, $\sin$, $\exp$, $|\cdot|$, or composition, and so is not merely *a* decidable fragment of $\mathcal{R}$ — it is disjoint from the generators that make $\mathcal{R}$ undecidable in the first place. This is the precise, provable version of the original document's §4 proposal to retreat to "domain-specific environments": it is not a hopeful heuristic, it is the only way to obtain a decidable comparator once any sufficiently rich set of transcendental generators is in play, and Theorem 6.1 tells you exactly where the line is.

### 6.1 A second island: trigonometric polynomials

The original document used $\sin^2(x)+\cos^2(x)=1$ as its illustration of "structure being needlessly thrown away." That identity sits formally inside Richardson's undecidable class $\mathcal{R}$ — yet it, and an entire useful sub-language around it, is in fact decidable and fits directly into the machinery above. The reason is an explicit ring embedding.

**Proposition 6.2.** Let $\mathcal{T}$ be the set of *trigonometric polynomials*: finite real linear combinations of $\sin(k x)$ and $\cos(k x)$ for $k\in\mathbb{Z}_{\ge0}$ (no nested transcendental composition — only linear arguments $kx$). Via Euler's formula, with $z=e^{ix}$,
$$\sin(kx) = \frac{z^{k}-z^{-k}}{2i}, \qquad \cos(kx)=\frac{z^{k}+z^{-k}}{2},$$
$\mathcal{T}$ embeds as a ring into the Laurent polynomial ring $\mathbb{C}[z,z^{-1}]$. Two elements of $\mathcal{T}$ are equal as functions of $x$ iff their images agree as Laurent polynomials in $z$.

A Laurent polynomial is just a polynomial in $z$ multiplied by some power $z^{-M}$, so equality testing reduces immediately to ordinary polynomial identity testing — Theorem 5.2 applies verbatim, sampling $z$ from a finite subset of $\mathbb{C}\setminus\{0\}$ (or, as in §7, from the roots of unity, for an exact rather than probabilistic test).

**Worked check.** $\sin^2x+\cos^2x$ becomes
$$\left(\frac{z-z^{-1}}{2i}\right)^2+\left(\frac{z+z^{-1}}{2}\right)^2 = \frac{-(z^2-2+z^{-2})}{4}+\frac{z^2+2+z^{-2}}{4}=\frac{4}{4}=1,$$
matching the constant Laurent polynomial $1$ — exactly the value of $2/2$. The framework of §§4–5 thus does not merely tolerate this example; it explains, mechanically and via a one-line computation, exactly why the original document's intuition about it was correct, and it does so without Claude — or any computer — ever "evaluating $\sin$ and $\cos$ at a number" in the way a calculator would.

**Where this island ends.** The embedding works because the only transcendental ingredient is $e^{ix}$ with a *linear* argument. The moment composition is nested — $\sin(\sin x)$, $x^{x}$, or $\pi\cdot e^{x}$ — one is back inside (or beyond) Richardson's class $\mathcal{R}$, and Theorem 6.1 applies: no algorithm, fingerprint-based or otherwise, can exist for the general case. This is a theorem, not an engineering shortfall; no amount of cleverness in the embedding repairs it, because the embedding's entire power came from forbidding exactly the operations that make $\mathcal{R}$ undecidable.

## 7. Exact Structure via Roots of Unity, and the Kernel-Method Bridge

Theorem 5.2 is probabilistic and only certifies *equality*, not a meaningful notion of distance between unequal expressions. This section gives a complementary, fully deterministic construction that does carry a genuine metric, at a higher (but still modest) sampling cost — and shows that both constructions are instances of one general principle from machine learning.

### 7.1 The exact isometry

**Theorem 7.1.** Let $p,q\in\mathbb{C}[x]$ have degree $<n$, with coefficient vectors $a,a'\in\mathbb{C}^n$. Let $\omega=e^{2\pi i/n}$ and define the evaluation vector $v_t=p(\omega^t)$, $t=0,\dots,n-1$ (i.e. $v=Fa$ where $F_{tj}=\omega^{tj}$ is the discrete Fourier transform matrix). Then $F^*F=nI$, and consequently
$$\langle v,v'\rangle = n\,\langle a,a'\rangle, \qquad \|v-v'\| = \sqrt{n}\,\|a-a'\|.$$

*Proof.* $(F^*F)_{j,j'}=\sum_{t=0}^{n-1}\omega^{t(j'-j)}$, a geometric series equal to $n$ when $j=j'$ and to $0$ otherwise (since $\omega^n=1$ and $0<|j-j'|<n$). Hence $F^*F=nI$, and $\langle v,v'\rangle=(Fa)^*(Fa')=a^*F^*Fa'=n\langle a,a'\rangle$; apply this to $a-a'$ for the norm statement. $\blacksquare$

In words: evaluating a polynomial at the $n$-th roots of unity is, up to a fixed rescaling, an *exact isometry* between the coefficient representation and the evaluation representation — not an approximation, not a high-probability statement. Equality is recovered exactly ($v=v'\iff p=p'$), and so is the Euclidean *distance* between non-equal polynomials, with respect to the standard monomial basis. The cost is $n=d+1$ deterministic evaluation points, in contrast to Theorem 5.2's $O(\log N)$ random points; one trades sample-efficiency for an exact, basis-relative metric rather than a mere equivalence test. (The result generalizes to several variables via the tensor/Kronecker product of one-dimensional DFTs, evaluating at the Cartesian grid of roots of unity in each variable; we omit the routine multivariate bookkeeping.)

**Honesty check.** This metric is canonical only *relative to the monomial basis*. Expressed in a different basis (Chebyshev, Bernstein, Lagrange-on-some-other-grid), the same polynomials receive different coefficient vectors and hence a different Euclidean distance. Theorem 7.1 gives *a* rigorous, computable, basis-relative metric — exactly as much as one is generally entitled to ask for in a setting where "structural distance" is not otherwise canonically defined. It does **not** give a single, basis-independent, intrinsic "Expression Metric" in the absolute sense the precursor document hoped for; no such object is canonically defined by the algebra alone, any more than "the" distance between two vectors is canonical without first fixing an inner product.

### 7.2 The kernel-method bridge

In statistical learning, the kernel trick computes $\langle\phi(x),\phi(y)\rangle$ for some (possibly infinite-dimensional) feature map $\phi$, using a closed-form kernel $K(x,y)$ that never constructs $\phi(x)$ explicitly (Aronszajn, 1950; Boser, Guyon & Vapnik, 1992). Theorem 7.1 and Theorem 5.2 are both special cases of exactly this move, with the feature map $\phi(p)=a$ (the coefficient tensor) and:

- **Theorem 7.1** as the *exact* kernel $K(p,q)=\frac{1}{n}\langle v_p,v_q\rangle$, computed via $n$ evaluations rather than by forming $a,a'$ directly;
- **Theorem 5.2** as a *randomized, equality-testing* kernel: $K(p,q)\approx \mathbb{1}[p=q]$, certified rather than computed exactly, using $O(\log N)$ evaluations instead of $n$.

This is the precise content behind the original document's appeal to "latent space" and neuro-symbolic embeddings: a learned neural embedding of expressions has no correctness guarantee, while the two constructions above are explicit, closed-form, and provably correct — a strictly stronger (if narrower) claim than what embedding-learning approaches typically offer.

## 8. What This Framework Provably Does Not Do

Stated plainly, in the order the precursor document raised its ambitions:

**(a) It does not eliminate scalars.** Both constructions still touch the field $F$ at specific points; what is eliminated is the *exhaustive expansion* step (building all $(d+1)^n$ coefficients, or all monomials of a circuit's output), not arithmetic itself. "Non-scalar computation," read literally, is not what is being offered; "non-exhaustive, structure-respecting computation" is, and that distinction matters for an honest abstract.

**(b) It does not produce a canonical Total Order.** Theorem 5.2 gives an equivalence relation; Theorem 7.1 gives a basis-relative metric. Neither gives a canonical *order* on inequivalent expressions analogous to the order on $\mathbb{R}$. One can always impose an order — e.g., lexicographically on fingerprint vectors, or by total degree, or by value at a fixed reference point — but every such order is a choice external to the algebra, not a fact the algebra hands you for free. The original document's repeated invocation of "Total Order" between arbitrary expressions is, in this sense, not realized here, and we do not believe it can be realized canonically; "bigger" and "smaller" are properties of ordered fields, not of polynomial rings in general.

**(c) It does not extend past Richardson's wall.** §6 is not a limitation of cleverness; Theorem 6.1 is a theorem. Any claim of a comparator that works for *all* expressions involving $\sin$, $\exp$, and composition is false, and we have been careful not to make it.

**(d) Removing the remaining randomness is, itself, a famous open problem.** Theorem 5.2's only imperfection is its (tunably tiny, but nonzero) failure probability. One might hope for a fully deterministic version with the same efficiency. This is precisely the *derandomization of PIT* problem, and:

> **Theorem 8.1 (Kabanets & Impagliazzo, 2003).** If there is an algorithm that decides, in polynomial time (or even nondeterministic sub-exponential time, infinitely often), whether a given arithmetic circuit over the integers computes the identically-zero polynomial, then either $\mathsf{NEXP}\not\subset\mathsf{P/poly}$, or the Permanent polynomial has no polynomial-size arithmetic circuits.

In other words: a fully deterministic, equally efficient version of Theorem 5.2 for *general* arithmetic circuits is not merely unknown — obtaining one would itself prove one of two major open lower bounds in complexity theory. This is the rigorous reason the word "revolutionary" must be used carefully here. The construction in this paper does not need or claim such a derandomization; it uses randomness honestly, with an explicit, user-chosen failure probability $\delta$, exactly as the sum-check protocol and every other randomized algorithm in this area does.

**(e) The representation is a choice, not a discovery.** The coefficient tensor depends on the monomial basis; the evaluation set $S$ and the sample size $k$ are parameters the user picks. We have proved that *for any* such valid choice, the stated guarantees hold — we have not found one true, parameter-free "Expression Metric" that the universe was hiding.

## 9. A Reference Algorithm, and a Note on Systems

For completeness, the comparator of §5 as pseudocode:

```
Algorithm ExpressionFingerprints(circuits C_1..C_N, degree bound d,
                                  field F, sample set S with |S| = m > d,
                                  confidence 1-δ)
    k ← ceil( ( ln C(N,2) + ln(1/δ) ) / ln(m/d) )
    sample r_1, ..., r_k independently, uniformly from S^n
    for i in 1..N:
        for t in 1..k:
            v[i][t] ← Evaluate(C_i, r_t)     # forward pass, O(size(C_i)) field ops,
                                              # division gates evaluated directly
    return v_1, ..., v_N
    # guarantee (Theorem 5.2): w.p. ≥ 1-δ,  v_i = v_j  <=>  [[C_i]] = [[C_j]],
    #            for every pair i, j, simultaneously
```

A practical consequence worth stating explicitly: because equality of fingerprint vectors implies (with the stated confidence) equality of the underlying expressions, the vectors $v_i$ can be hashed and stored in an ordinary hash table, turning "is this new expression already known under another name?" into an $O(1)$ amortized lookup rather than an $O(N)$ pairwise re-comparison. This is, in spirit, exactly the bookkeeping already performed by *e-graph*-based equality-saturation systems (Tate, Stepp, Tatlock & Lerner, 2009; the `egg` library, Willsey et al., 2021), which maintain large, growing sets of equivalent expressions for use in compiler optimization and term rewriting. The contribution here is not a new system, but a closed-form, provably correct fingerprint that such a system could use in place of (or alongside) its existing congruence-closure machinery, with an explicit, tunable probability of error rather than an implicit one.

## 10. Discussion: Mapping Back to the Original Vision

| Original (informal) claim | Rigorous status in this paper | Where it stops |
|---|---|---|
| Build an "Expression Metric": compare $N$ expressions without computing $N$ scalars | **Proved** (Thm 5.2, Thm 7.1): two explicit, correct constructions, costing $O(\log N)$ or $O(d)$ evaluations respectively | Both still use scalar evaluations as probes; "no scalars at all" is not achieved, nor achievable as literally stated |
| "Look at it and see," near-zero computation, because the environment is good enough | **Proved, quantitatively** (Cor. 5.3): query count is *logarithmic* in $N$ and independent of expression size | Each probe still costs $O(\text{circuit size})$; "zero computation" was always rhetorical, the honest version is "exponentially cheaper than expansion" |
| $+,-,\times,\div$ generality is rescued by recasting as convolution / dot product | **Proved** (Thm 4.2–4.4): exact, classical identities | Naive symbolic clearing of denominators can still blow up (§3.2); the efficient route evaluates circuits directly (Remark 5.4) |
| Avoid Richardson's barrier via domain-specific environments | **Proved, with an exact boundary** (Thm 6.1, Prop. 6.2): rational expressions and trigonometric polynomials are explicit decidable islands | Nested composition with $\sin,\exp,|\cdot|$ remains undecidable, period — this is a theorem, not a heuristic gap |
| Category theory: functors as relation-detectors | **Made precise** (§5.1): evaluation homomorphisms, from the universal property of polynomial rings | A real but narrow instance of "functorial," not a new categorical framework |
| Latent-space / neuro-symbolic embeddings | **Replaced with something stronger** (§7.2): two explicit, closed-form, provably correct embeddings | These are not learned and carry no claim about *semantic* similarity beyond algebraic equality and basis-relative distance |
| "This is genuinely revolutionary, fully solving the problem" | **Tempered, deliberately** (§8, Thm 8.1) | The individual tools are 20–60 years old; the assembly and the exact-boundary framing are, to our knowledge, the contribution; claiming more would fail review |

What is, to the authors' knowledge, specifically new in this paper rather than restated from prior art: (i) the explicit "Expression Fingerprint" theorem (Thm 5.2) with its logarithmic-in-$N$ query bound, stated for a *growing collection* of expressions rather than a single equality test, together with the hash-table consequence in §9; (ii) the side-by-side pairing of the probabilistic fingerprint (Thm 5.2) and the exact DFT isometry (Thm 7.1) as two ends of one accuracy/cost trade-off; (iii) the explicit Laurent-polynomial embedding of trigonometric identities (Prop. 6.2) as a second worked "island," directly addressing the example the precursor document itself raised; and (iv) the single table above, translating an informal research program into falsifiable claims. None of these overturns a known impossibility result, and none should be read as doing so.

## 11. Conclusion

The dream in the precursor document — a computer that relates to expressions the way a physical system relates to interacting fields, structurally, without first collapsing everything to a number — is not a fantasy. It is true, exactly, on two charted islands: the field of rational functions under $+,-,\times,\div$, and the ring of trigonometric polynomials under linear-argument $\sin/\cos$. On those islands, comparison can be done by touching a logarithmic number of points of a circuit that is never expanded, or by an exact isometry that touches $d+1$ points and never loses a bit of distance information. Both facts are theorems, proved above from ingredients that are individually fifty years old and have simply never, so far as we know, been assembled and bounded quite this way for the specific purpose of comparing a growing collection of expressions.

Beyond those islands lies the open sea Richardson mapped in 1968: expressions built freely from $\sin$, $\exp$, $\pi$, and composition, where "is this expression zero" is not merely hard but *undecidable* — no algorithm, present or future, fingerprint-based or otherwise, can settle it in general. That is not a failure of this paper's ambition; it is the most important fact this paper can report, because a whitepaper that promised to cross that sea would not survive the first page of review, and would not deserve to.
